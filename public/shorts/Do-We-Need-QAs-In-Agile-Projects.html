Parsh's Testing Wiki - Do We Need QAs In Agile?


YES and NO


What does a QA bring to the table:

  1. A Question Asker, challenges assumptions, highlights ambiguity and anomalies, clarifies scope.
  2. A focus on the detail and not just the 'big picture', along with a focus on behaviour rather than technology
  3. Scenario generating machine, ideal for Story Refinement sessions
  4. Bug finder extraordinaire, drawing on instinct, suspicion, and experience
  5. Testing tools expertise eg, automation, investigation, simulation etcetera

But they also come at a cost:

  1. An extra team resource to hire, particularly as good QAs are a scarce resource
  2. Can become a process bottlekneck
  3. Can encourage a decoupling of Quality from the SDLC

So when should you definitely get a QA:

     If you have a medium-high risk / medium-high impact application (eg. health, finance, government)

            OR

     A weak agile process, or a process that is honoured more in the breach than the observation


So when should you think twice before getting a dedicated QA:

     If you have a low-medium risk / low-medium impact application and can tolerate some regressions

            AND

     A strong agile process supported by the guiding hand of a Scrum Master

            AND

     You can fallback releases quickly


     nb. Not having a dedicated QA doesn't mean that we don't Exploratory Test. An independent team member should take on this important task.


What is a strong Agile process... in brief, one that has; a solid Definition of Ready and Done, easy collaboration, lane discipline, shift-left mentality etc is a good starting point.


Understanding your exposure to risk and impact, and the strength of your agile culture should guide you to the useful assesment.
